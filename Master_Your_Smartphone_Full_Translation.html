I. Basics (Chapters 0-6)

Your phone is far more powerful than you could ever imagine. This section is the foundation for all the "magical operations" that follow. We will first unify our "language," and then step by step help you get to know and control your device.

Chapter 0: Hi, Before We Begin

Before we officially start exploring, let's talk about some basic rules to make sure you understand everything that follows~

1. What Can This Book Help You With? (And What It Cannot Do)

It can help you systematically understand how to use your phone, grasp the principles behind the functions, and most of the ideas apply whether you're an Android user or an Apple user. It will guide you through hands-on exercises.

It is not a manual for a specific phone model, nor is it tech news about chasing the latest products, and definitely not a guide to help you become addicted to your phone.

2. Our "Code" Explanations

To speak more clearly, this book uses some small markers:

  • [Button Name]: Represents a specific button or app name on the screen.
  • >: Represents the sequence of operations, for example "Tap [Settings] > [Sound]".
  • Bold: Very important! May be key concepts or safety warnings.
  • Italic: Some supplementary thoughts or metaphors.

3. Android and iOS, We Cover Both

Guess what? Some things have different names but do the same thing. For example: "Applications" (Android) and App (iOS), Settings (both called Settings), File Manager (Android) and Files (iOS). As long as you know they're the same thing, that's all that matters.

4. Phone Storage: Why Did You Buy 128GB But Only Have Less Than 110GB Available?

Storage units: 1TB = 1024GB, 1GB = 1024MB...

But the nominal capacity you bought becomes "smaller" after you get it. The main reasons are:

  1. The system takes up space: The operating system is like the load-bearing wall of a house—it must exist.
  2. Pre-installed apps from the manufacturer: The apps that come with a new phone also take up space.
  3. Formatted capacity loss: For technical reasons, like how a backpack's lining also takes up volume.

So when buying a phone, remember to factor in what the system takes up!

Hands-On Time: Check Your Phone's "Identity Card"

Open [Settings], find "About Phone" (or similar option), and note down: Phone model, system version, total storage space, and remaining space. This is the first step to understanding it.

Chapter 1: Hello, My Personal Computer

Your phone is actually the most personal supercomputer in your pocket.

1. Your Phone's Hardware: The "Five Viscera and Six Bowels"

  1. SoC (Chip): The phone's "brain + heart," determining performance.
  2. RAM (Running Memory): A temporary workbench—the larger it is, the smoother it is to have multiple apps open at the same time.
  3. Storage (ROM): A long-term warehouse where all photos, videos, and apps are stored.
  4. Screen: Your eyes. What matters is type (OLED has vibrant colours, LCD is easier on the eyes), resolution (clarity), and refresh rate (whether scrolling feels responsive).
  5. Battery and Charging: Charge whenever you use it—don't wait until it's completely dead or keep it at 100% all the time, that's the most friendly to the battery. Fast charging and wireless charging are very convenient.
  6. Camera: Now relies on multiple lenses + algorithms (computational photography) working together—night scenes and bokeh all depend on it.
  7. Sensors: Your phone's small feelers. Like gyroscope (game steering), NFC (swiping access cards and transit cards), proximity sensor (auto screen-off during calls).

2. Operating System: Android VS iOS

  • Android: Like an open-source community—phone manufacturers can deeply customise it, offering more choices.
  • iOS: Like a well-tended garden—Apple controls both hardware and software, creating a unified experience.

Both ecosystems are extremely rich—just choose the lifestyle you like.

3. Your Phone's Many Roles

It has long stopped being just a phone: It is a communication tool, smart camera, wallet, information portal, gaming console, mobile office, health assistant, smart home remote control, and even your digital ID card.

Hands-On Time: Understand Hardware Configuration

In [Settings], see if you can find the processor model, RAM, and ROM sizes. Get a sense of your phone's capabilities.

Chapter 2: Organising Your Digital Room — File Management

Phones get messy after long use—good habits start with managing files.

1. The Three-Layer Storage System

  1. Internal Storage: The core area, fast speed—operating system and apps are all here.
  2. SD Card (if supported): An extended wardrobe, slower speed—suitable for movies and photos.
  3. Cloud Storage: A safe + sync network drive. Prevents loss and enables synchronisation between multiple devices.

2. Getting to Know the "File Manager"

Android usually has a [Files] or [File Manager] app, iOS has the built-in [Files] app. They are your "resource managers" for viewing all files on your phone. You will see folders like Download, DCIM (camera photos), Pictures, etc.

3. Common File Operations

  • Create folder: Long-press > New folder.
  • Move files: Long-press > Select > Move to / Cut.
  • Bulk selection: Long-press to enter selection mode, then tap to select multiple items.
  • Sort: Usually by name, date, size—choose what suits you.

4. Cleaning Up: Where to Start?

  1. Duplicate photos: Use your phone's built-in cleanup tool or third-party apps to identify and remove duplicates.
  2. Screenshots and long screenshots: Create a dedicated folder to manage them—they accumulate quickly!
  3. Old videos: Back up important ones to the cloud or computer, then delete to free up space.
  4. Unused apps: Uninstall apps you haven't used in months.

Hands-On Time: Clean Up Your Phone's "Digital Room"

Goal: Find your File Manager, create a new folder, move some old screenshots into it, and then use the storage cleanup tool to scan once.

Chapter 3: Connecting Your Phone to the "Lifeline" — Network

No network, no phone—let's get your phone online.

1. Mobile Data: Your Anywhere Internet

  • Data usage monitoring: Check in [Settings] > [Network] or [Data Usage]—set limits to avoid overspending.
  • Data saving: Close background app data usage, restrict certain apps from using mobile data.
  • When to use: Wi-Fi is available at home and work; mobile data is for going out.

2. Wi-Fi: Home Internet

  • Auto-connect: Enable this for home and work Wi-Fi—convenient but be careful about security.
  • Public Wi-Fi: Be especially cautious—avoid accessing sensitive apps like banking.
  • Wi-Fi troubleshooting: If connected but no internet, try forgetting the network and reconnecting, or restart the router.

3. Bluetooth: The Wireless Connector

Its uses: Pairing headphones, speakers, smartwatches, car systems, etc.

  • Pairing: Enable Bluetooth on both devices, search and connect on one side.
  • Unpairing: Go to Bluetooth settings, find the device, and tap to disconnect or forget.
  • Tips: Turn off Bluetooth when not in use to save power.

4. Flight Mode and Network Troubles

Flight mode turns off all wireless connections—useful on planes or when you need to focus.

Network troubleshooting: Restart phone > Check if mobile data is on > Check if Wi-Fi is working > Contact operator if problems persist.

Hands-On Time: Check Your Data Usage

In [Settings], find "Data Usage" or "Network." See how much data you've used this month and which apps consume the most data.

Chapter 4: Installing and Managing Apps — Your Phone's "Skill Pack"

Apps are your phone's superpowers—let's learn to manage them wisely.

1. Where to Download Apps?

  • Official sources first: Apple's App Store, Android's built-in app store (Huawei AppGallery, Xiaomi App Store, etc.).
  • Why not unknown sources?: Unofficial sources may contain malware—safety first!

2. App Permissions: What Are They?

Apps ask for permissions to use certain phone functions. For example, a camera app needs camera and storage permissions.

  • Common permissions: Storage, camera, microphone, location, contacts.
  • Principle: "Does this app really need this permission?" If not, deny it.
  • How to check: [Settings] > [Apps] > [Permissions].

3. App Management

  • Updates: Keep apps updated for new features and security patches.
  • Uninstall: Long-press the app icon, tap uninstall—or drag to the uninstall area.
  • Disable: For pre-installed apps you don't need, you can disable them (not delete, just hide).

4. App Versions and Updates

Why update? New features, performance improvements, and security bug fixes.

How to update: Go to app store, check for updates, or enable auto-update.

Hands-On Time: Review App Permissions

Go to [Settings] > [Apps] > [Permissions]. Check the permissions for 3-5 frequently used apps. Are there any permissions that seem unnecessary?

Chapter 5: Making Your Phone Understand You — Basic Settings

A few simple settings can make your phone much easier to use.

1. Display Settings

  • Brightness: Set to auto-brightness—the phone adjusts based on ambient light.
  • Dark mode: Switch between light and dark themes—easier on the eyes at night.
  • Font size: Adjust to a comfortable reading size.
  • Screen timeout: Set how long until the screen auto-locks.

2. Sound and Vibration

  • Ringtone: Set your favourite ringtone.
  • Notification sounds: Different apps can have different sounds.
  • Do Not Disturb: Silence all notifications—great for sleep or focus time.

3. Notifications Management

Too many notifications are distracting. We need to take control:

  • Turn off notifications for apps that aren't important.
  • Allow only important notifications to break through Do Not Disturb.
  • Notification badges: Those little red numbers—clean them up!

4. Security Settings

  • Lock screen password: Set a secure password/PIN/pattern.
  • Fingerprint/Face unlock: Convenient and relatively secure.
  • Find My Phone: Must-enable feature (discussed in detail in Chapter 6).

Hands-On Time: Customise Your Notification Centre

Pull down the notification centre. Are there notification toggles you don't need? Turn them off. Find [Settings] > [Notifications] and refine which apps are allowed to send notifications.

Chapter 6: Protecting Your Digital Life — Security and Privacy

Your phone holds so much personal information—let's learn to protect it.

6.1 Lock Screen: Your First Line of Defense

  • Password/PIN: The most basic security measure—don't use "123456"!
  • Pattern: Draw a pattern to unlock—easy to remember but be careful about smudges.
  • Fingerprint: Fast and secure.
  • Face recognition: Convenient, but some models have security weaknesses—evaluate based on your situation.

6.2 "Find My Phone": Your Phone's Lifeline

This feature is most important when you lose your phone:

  • Location: See where your phone is on a map.
  • Play sound: Make your phone ring—great for finding it at home.
  • Lost mode: Remotely lock the phone and display a contact number—someone who finds it can contact you.
  • Remote erase: The last resort—clears all data (but you can no longer locate it after erasing).

Go to [Settings]—Apple users find "Find My iPhone," Android users find "Find My Device" or similar brand service—make sure it's turned on (green).

6.3 System Updates: Patch Holes

The primary purpose of system updates is fixing security vulnerabilities—it's like patching holes in your house. So when you see a system update prompt, try to upgrade as soon as possible.

6.4 Recognising and Preventing Telecom Fraud

  • Common scams: Impersonating police/customers (phishing), winning prizes/刷单 rebates (bait), online romance scams (pig butchering scam).
  • Built-in phone protection: Harassment call blocking, verification code theft prevention, SMS link security checks—remember to turn these on in settings.
  • Anti-fraud mantra: Don't easily trust, don't disclose, don't click, don't transfer, verify first if in doubt!

Hands-On Time: Enable and Test "Remote Lock"

  1. Make sure "Find My Phone" is enabled.
  2. On a computer browser or another phone, log in to the corresponding cloud service (iCloud.com or brand cloud service website).
  3. Find "Find Device" function, try issuing a "Play Sound" or simulated "Lock" command to your own phone—familiarise yourself with the entire operation process so you won't panic in critical moments.

II. Software (Chapters 7-12)

Chapter 7: Communication — SMS, Calls and Social

Communication is the phone's most primitive and essential purpose. Now it has evolved from simple calls and SMS into a complex ecosystem including instant messaging, social networks, and email.

7.1 Phone App Advanced Features: More Than Just Making Calls

Guess what? Modern phone dialers have long surpassed simple keypads, integrating powerful tools like call recording, spam blocking, and smart identification.

1. Call Recording (Note Legal Compliance):
  • Purpose: Record important call content for work notes, interviews, customer commitments, or dispute preservation.
  • Legal note: In China, using call recordings as evidence requires obtaining the other party's consent, or using them to protect legitimate rights (like complaining about harassment or fraud). Before commercial or sensitive conversations, verbally informing the other party "This call may be recorded" is the safer approach.
  • How to operate:
    • Android: Usually has a clear "Record" button in the call interface. You can find "Call Recording" option in the phone app settings and set auto-recording rules.
    • iOS: The system does not provide native call recording function. If needed, use third-party call recording apps.
2. Spam Call Blocking and Smart Identification:
  • Principle: The phone app queries a large number database when calls come in, displaying "Spam Call," "Scam Call," or company name.
  • How to enable:
    • Android: Enter Phone app > Upper right "Settings" > Spam blocking or Smart identification. Enable all identification and blocking options.
    • iOS: Enter [Settings] > [Phone] > [Call Blocking & Identification]. You can see installed third-party security apps (like Tencent Phone Manager) that support this function, and allow them to work. Enabling "Silence Unknown Callers" mutes all calls not in your contacts.
  • Tip: After receiving a spam call, actively mark it as "Spam" or "Scam" in call history. This contributes data to the database and helps others—don't get it wrong!
3. Call Assistance Features:
  • Call recording to text: Some Android brand phones can, after call recording, directly provide a "Convert to Text" button next to the recording file, turning dialogue into text.
  • Visual assistance (Live Captions): In Android accessibility settings or iOS hearing accessibility, you can enable "Live Transcribe" to generate real-time captions for all audio including calls.

7.2 SMS vs iMessage/RCS: Verification Codes and Spam Filtering

Traditional SMS has not been eliminated—it remains a reliable channel for receiving verification codes, bank notifications, and official information.

1. SMS vs iMessage (iOS) / RCS (Android):
  • iMessage: Messages sent between Apple devices via internet, displayed as blue bubbles. Supports high-quality images and videos, read receipts, completely free and end-to-end encrypted.
  • RCS: The next-generation communication protocol promoted by global carriers and Google. Functions similar to iMessage or WeChat but based on phone numbers. Check and enable "Chat Features" in Android messaging app settings—don't get it wrong!
2. Verification Code Security:

The system automatically identifies and categorises verification code SMS. Never disclose verification codes to anyone.

3. Spam Filtering:
  • iOS: [Settings] > [Messages], enable "Filter Unknown Senders."
  • Android: In messaging app settings, enable "Smart Messaging" or "Spam Filtering."
  • General tip: For obvious promotional spam, do not reply to anything (including "TD to unsubscribe"). Just delete or mark as spam. Replying sometimes confirms your number is valid.

7.3 WeChat/QQ: National-Level Social App Management

WeChat and QQ have surpassed being mere communication tools—they have become "super apps" integrating social, payment, and information acquisition.

1. Chat History Backup and Migration:
  • Importance: Chat history contains important files, memories, and work information, but is only stored locally and in server temporary cache. Losing your phone or uninstalling and reinstalling causes permanent loss of records.
  • Backup to computer (most reliable):
    1. Log into PC or Mac WeChat on computer.
    2. Click bottom left menu > [Migrate & Backup] > [Backup & Restore].
    3. Select "Backup Chat History to Computer." On phone, select chats to backup.
    4. Note: Backup files are encrypted and stored locally on your computer. For restore, phone and computer need to be on the same Wi-Fi.
2. Storage Space Cleanup:
  • Path: WeChat [Me] > [Settings] > [General] > [Storage Space]. QQ [Settings] > [General] > [Storage Space Management].
  • Cleanup strategy:
    1. Cache: Safe to clear—temporary data generated by app operation.
    2. Chat history: Enter to carefully clean by time, file type, or individual chats. For example, clean all videos older than one year across all chats in one go.
3. Privacy and Security Settings:
  • Ways to add me: Control how others find you (phone number, WeChat ID, group chat, etc.).
  • Moments permissions: Set who can see, do not show to certain people.
  • Authorization management: In WeChat [Settings] > [Personal Info & Permissions] > [Authorization Management], view and remove third-party website or app WeChat login authorizations.

7.4 Email: Configuring and Using on Mobile

Efficiently handling email on mobile is a key part of mobile office.

1. Adding Email Accounts:
  • System mail app:
    • iOS: [Settings] > [Mail] > [Accounts] > [Add Account].
    • Android: System usually has "Email" app. Open and follow prompts to add.
  • Dedicated client apps: Like Gmail, Outlook—optimised for specific email services with more powerful features.
2. Mobile Email Tips:
  • Push notification management: Set different notification methods for different email accounts.
  • Gesture operations: Most email apps support swipe gestures to quickly archive, delete, or flag emails.
  • Signature: Set a professional mobile email signature.
3. Security Notes:

Be cautious about logging into email or sending sensitive emails on public Wi-Fi.

Chapter 13: Battery Science and Power Optimisation

The battery is your phone's "energy heart." This chapter helps you understand the battery's quirks, learn scientific charging and battery life tips, and completely say goodbye to battery anxiety.

13.1 How Does Battery Work? Cycles and Health

Phone batteries (lithium-ion or lithium-polymer) are like energy sponges: charging is absorbing water (storing energy), discharging is squeezing water (using energy). Remember three key characteristics:

  • Charge whenever: Different from old batteries—it has no memory effect, does not need to be drained completely before charging. Draining to 0% or keeping at 100% all the time actually causes damage.
  • Hates heat: High temperature is the battery's number one killer.
  • Ages over time: Maximum capacity decreases with use—this is normal.
Core indicators: Cycle Count and Health
  • One cycle is not one charge. For example, using 60% today and fully charging, then using 40% tomorrow totals 100% consumption—that is one cycle.
  • Battery health is current maximum capacity as a percentage of when new. Below 80%, you will noticeably feel shorter battery life—consider official battery replacement.
  • How to check:
    • iPhone: Settings > Battery > Battery Health and Charging.
    • Android: Generally search "battery health" in settings.

13.2 Charging Myths? Fast Charging, Wireless Charging and Golden Rules

Busting rumours, charging scientifically.

1. Does fast charging damage battery?

Using original or certified fast-charging accessories causes minimal damage. Phone and charger have temperature control—will automatically reduce power if overheating. Do not use off-brand fast chargers.

2. How about wireless charging?
  • Convenient: Charge anytime—great for overnight charging.
  • Drawbacks: Lower efficiency, more heat, slower charging.
  • Suggestion: Good for overnight charging (trickle charging); use wired fast charging when in a hurry. Remove thick phone case when using.
3. Golden health rules for battery
  • Optimal battery level: Try to keep between 20%-80%—do not let it starve (0%) or stuff itself (100%).
  • Is overnight charging safe? Yes! Automatically stops when full—phone draws power directly from outlet.
  • Never play heavy games while charging—combined heat is very damaging.
  • Storing phone long-term: Charge to around 50% before powered-off storage.

13.3 Real Battery-Saving Tips

Battery health alone is not enough—settings are the big battery-saving moves.

1. Find the "Battery Hog"
  • Screen (biggest consumer): Lower brightness, shorten auto-lock time. OLED screen phones must enable dark mode—it saves significant battery.
  • Poor signal: In elevators, basements—phone frantically searches for signal, draining battery fast.
  • 5G: If signal is poor, manually switch back to 4G—battery life much better.
  • Location services:
    • Non-navigation apps (e.g., social, shopping)—set location permission to "Only While Using."
    • Turn off non-essential options like "Location-Based Ads" in system location settings.
2. Manage apps' appetites
  • Restrict background activity:
    • iPhone: Settings > General > Background App Refresh—can turn all off or leave only WeChat.
    • Android: In app battery settings, select "Restrict Background Activity" for unused apps.
  • Turn off unnecessary notifications: Every screen-on and vibration consumes battery.
3. Lifeline: Battery Saver Mode
  • Battery Saver/Low Power Mode: System reduces performance, stops background sync—effective for temporary emergency.
  • Ultra Battery Saver/Extreme Mode: Only phone, SMS and a few apps remain—screen turns black and white, can squeeze the last bit of battery for emergency use.

Hands-On Exercise: Give Your Battery a Health Check

Goal: Use data to find the real battery drain culprits.

  1. Check health: Following the method above, find and note your battery's maximum capacity.
  2. Analyse power details:
    • iPhone: Go to Settings > Battery—check chart and app list below, focus on which apps have long background activity.
    • Android: Go to Settings > Battery > Battery Usage—check hardware and app power consumption ranking.
  3. Formulate strategy:
    • If an app has abnormal background drain → Restrict it.
    • If "Mobile Network" power consumption is high → Recall if you often stay in areas with poor signal.
    • Find uncommon apps on the list → Consider uninstalling.

Chapter 14: Account System and Cloud Services

Your account is your phone's "identity card"—cloud services are your "digital safe deposit box."

14.1 Why Account Systems Matter

  • Account = Your digital identity in the ecosystem (Apple ID, Google Account, brand account).
  • Losing your account means losing purchases, cloud data, and find-my-phone features.
  • Protect your account like protecting your wallet.

14.2 Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Enable 2FA for all important accounts.

  • Recommended: Use authentication app (Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator) instead of SMS.
  • Save backup codes in a secure place.

Chapter 18: Deep Dive into Mobile Networks and Information Security

🔒 Why do we need to care about this?

We use phones for internet, payments, chatting every day—it feels so convenient. But have you thought about how exposed your chat logs, payment passwords, and photos are when they are "running naked" on the internet? Your phone is your digital avatar—its security is your own security. Do not think hackers are far away—one careless move with public Wi-Fi might make you "step on a mine." Today, let us dig deep into the "undercurrents" in mobile networks and make your phone steady as a mountain, do not get this wrong.

📡 VPN: The "Invisible Cloak" in the Network

What exactly is it?

VPN stands for Virtual Private Network. You can imagine it as building a dedicated, encrypted underground tunnel between your home and the website you want to visit.

  • Normal browsing: Your phone -> Public network (operator network, cafe Wi-Fi) -> Target website.

    Risk: Along this road, someone (hacker, malicious Wi-Fi provider, even network administrator) might be "watching" the data you send.

  • Using VPN: Your phone -> VPN encrypted tunnel -> VPN server -> Target website.

    Effect: All your network traffic, from entering the tunnel, is encrypted and encapsulated. "Peepers" along the way only see garbled text. Only when data reaches VPN server is it decrypted and forwarded to the real website. The website sees the VPN server's address, not your real address.

What can it do? Besides "circumventing censorship"

Do not think of VPN only for accessing foreign websites—it has many legitimate uses:

  1. Protect security on public Wi-Fi: Airports, hotels, cafes using free Wi-Fi are high-risk areas for data leakage. Using VPN armours your connection.
  2. Secure remote access to company intranet: Many company employees working from home access company's internal systems and file servers securely through VPN.
  3. Protect personal privacy, avoid tracking: Prevent your Internet Service Provider (ISP) from recording and analysing your browsing habits to deliver targeted ads.

⚠️ An important reminder

Although VPN technology itself is a legitimate security tool, be sure to purchase from official channels or use reputable services. Do not use those dubious "free VPNs"—they might plant Trojans in your phone, or secretly record and sell your data—that is like "hiring a thief as a security guard."

🚫 The "Sweet Trap" of Public Wi-Fi

Free Wi-Fi is tempting, but may be "sugar-coated bullets." Besides using VPN as the "ultimate solution," we can build two basic defence lines.

  • First defence line: Recognise HTTPS

    When visiting a website, check if the address starts with http:// or https://. That extra "s" stands for Secure. It means communication between you and the website is encrypted—even if data is intercepted, it is hard to crack. Today, vast majority of legitimate websites (banks, email, social platforms) use HTTPS by default. On public Wi-Fi, be sure the page you are visiting is HTTPS.

  • Second defence line: Upgrade your DNS query

    What is DNS? Simply put, it is the "network phonebook." When you want to access example.com, your phone first asks DNS server: "What is the address (IP) of example.com?" Traditional DNS queries are like "postcards"—unencrypted. This means malicious Wi-Fi can see which websites you queried, and even tamper with answers, directing you to phishing websites!

    DNS over TLS (DoT) / DNS over HTTPS (DoH) solve this problem. They encrypt your "phonebook" queries too—so no one knows who you are looking up, and cannot change the answers.

🕵️ Can "Incognito Browsing" Really Make You Invisible?

All browsers have "Incognito/Private Browsing Mode"—does it make you feel like an internet ninja? The truth might disappoint you.

What does it actually do?

  • 👍 Does not save local history: After closing incognito window, system does not save your browsing history, cookies and form data. Someone picking up your phone cannot see what you just searched.

What it cannot do:

  1. Hide your browsing from your ISP, network administrator (if using company Wi-Fi), and websites can still identify you through your IP address and other technical means.
  2. Prevent viruses and malware: If you accidentally click into a malicious website, you will still get infected.
  3. Hide downloaded files: Files you download in incognito mode are still saved in your download folder.

Conclusion: Incognito mode is more like a "local eraser" than a "network invisibility cloak." It is suitable for protecting your login state and search records from being seen when borrowing someone else's computer—but do not rely on it to escape all network tracking.

Hands-On Time: Configure a "Trusted Phonebook" (DNS) for Your Phone

Now that we know DNS queries can be snooped and tampered with, let us change to a more secure, faster DNS service. Using Android and iOS as examples.

Android phone settings:

  1. Open [Settings].
  2. Go to [WLAN], long-press your current Wi-Fi network, select [Modify Network].
  3. Tap [Advanced Options], change IP Setting from "DHCP" to "Static."
  4. Scroll down, find "Domain Name 1" and "Domain Name 2" (DNS server addresses).
  5. Enter the DNS address you want, for example:
    • More security and privacy focused: Cloudflare DNS 1.1.1.1 and 1.0.0.1
    • Balanced experience: Google DNS 8.8.8.8 and 8.8.4.4
    • Domestic options: Alibaba DNS 223.5.5.5 and 223.6.6.6 / Tencent DNS 119.29.29.29
  6. Save settings.

iPhone/iPad Settings (in Wi-Fi environment):

  1. Open [Settings] > [Wi-Fi].
  2. Tap the [i] icon next to your connected Wi-Fi.
  3. Scroll down, find [Configure DNS].
  4. Select [Manual], tap [Add Server].
  5. Enter the recommended DNS address above (e.g., 1.1.1.1).
  6. Tap [Save] in top right.

After completion, your phone will prioritise the more trusted DNS server you set when querying website addresses—safer browsing and possibly faster response. Congratulations—you have plugged another security hole!

The previous chapter said your phone is your avatar—data leaving it faces risks. 🤖 AI Camera: Your "Darkroom" Moved Into Your Phone

Point-and-shoot might be the AI feature we use most. You think "taking photos" is just one click? Not anymore.

Early phone photography was like using a point-and-shoot film camera—the quality of the shot almost entirely depended on sensor and lens hardware. But now, AI computational photography is more like shoving a professional photo retoucher + photography team directly into the shutter button.

What is the difference?

This "team" is already working before you press the shutter. Scanning the scene, identifying "This is blue sky," "This is a face," "This is a night scene," or "This is a plate of food." After identification, AI instantly calls up an optimisation scheme carefully trained for this type of scene:

  • Shooting portraits: It knows to blur the background, beautify skin tone, automatically switch to portrait mode.
  • Shooting night scenes: It does not just blindly increase ISO causing a photo full of noise—instead it might quickly take multiple low-light photos, then like putting together a jigsaw puzzle, "computationally" fuses the clearest, least noisy parts from all photos into one bright, clean image.
  • Shooting documents: It automatically straightens edges, increases contrast—like a scanner.
  • Shooting action shots: It can predict motion trajectory, automatically capture the sharpest moment.

Hands-On Time: Open your phone camera now, aim at different scenes (faces, green plants, food, text), and carefully observe the viewfinder. Many phones, when recognising specific scenes, show a tiny icon hint in the corner of the viewfinder, like "Portrait," "Food," "Text." Feel this "instant recognition" process. This is AI giving you real-time feedback before you press the shutter.

So now, pressing the shutter is really just starting the last step of this "computational retouching" process. That one-second "Processing" spinner is AI brain working at full speed. What is great is, more and more of this complex computation is happening directly on the dedicated AI chip in your phone (like Apple's Neural Engine, Qualcomm AI Engine, Huawei's Da Vinci architecture, etc.)—fast and privacy-preserving.

🗣️ Voice Assistant: How Does Your "Voice Secretary" Understand You?

"Hey Siri, what is the weather tomorrow?"

"Xiaoai, set an alarm for 7 AM tomorrow."

This has become as natural as breathing. But have you ever wondered how this voice secretary works?

Its workflow can be broken down into a standardised "three-step process":

  1. Step 1: Hear (ASR - Automatic Speech Recognition).

    When you say the wake word ("Hey Siri") and give an instruction, the phone's microphone records your voice waveform—a continuous signal. AI's first task is to chop this continuous sound wave into individual phoneme fragments, then match them against its massive trained vocabulary to convert them into text. This is called ASR. It must overcome your accent, speech speed, and ambient noise—very impressive.

  2. Step 2: Understand (NLU - Natural Language Understanding).

    Getting the text "7 AM tomorrow remind me to take an umbrella" is not enough—it needs to understand the intent. Who? What time? Do what? This requires NLP technology. It analyses sentence structure, extracting key entities: "7 AM tomorrow" (time), "remind" (intent/action), "me" (object), "take umbrella" (event). This is understanding your command—important stuff.

  3. Step 3: Execute and Respond.

    After understanding your intent, the assistant calls the corresponding app or service module in your phone. If checking weather, it fetches weather data online; if setting an alarm, it writes to the "Clock" app database; if asking "Who is Messi?", it might call a search engine. Finally, it uses TTS (Text-to-Speech) technology to "speak" the result back to you.

In the past, most heavy computation for Steps 1 and 2 needed to be sent to cloud servers via the internet. But now, wake word recognition and some offline commands (like turning off lights) can be completely processed locally on your phone. This is faster (no waiting for network) and more private (commands like "turn off the lights" do not need to be uploaded to the cloud).

⌨️ Keyboard Predictions and Writing Assistance: Your "Considerate Stenographer"

This might be the most subtly pervasive AI. You just typed "tonight" and it jumps to "what to eat"; you input "boss" and "hello" automatically follows. Behind this are on-device machine learning models observing and learning from you over time.

  • Personalised language model based on your habits: The keyboard quietly analyses your high-frequency words, phrase collocations, and chat partners. You like using "hahaha" with friends, but use "meeting minutes" with colleagues—it remembers all this. Over time, it forms a prediction model optimised only for you.
  • Context understanding: It is not just looking at the previous word—it understands the context of the sentence you are typing. In a WeChat chat box versus a work document, the suggestions it gives for the same starting word might be completely different.
  • Error correction: When you type a wrong character, it knows you probably meant a different word—and automatically corrects. This is not just dictionary matching—it is probabilistic judgement trained on hundreds of millions of inputs.

🖋️ Going further: AI writing assistance.

Now many keyboards or note apps offer "smart correction," "sentence polishing," "continuation" features—these are more powerful AI applications. It can help polish "this product is quite good to use" into "this product offers an excellent user experience," or after you write a title, suggest possible article openings. This model is usually larger and may partially rely on the cloud, but core association and prediction still largely happens locally to protect your writing privacy.

🧠 On-Device AI vs Cloud AI: A Crossroads in Phone Evolution

We have actually been mentioning a key term repeatedly: on-device. This represents the core trend in phone AI development: computing moving closer to the user.

We can use a simple table to see the fundamental differences between "on-device AI" and traditional "cloud AI":

Comparison Dimension Cloud AI (Traditional) On-Device AI (Current & Future Trend)
Where does data go? Your photos, recordings and other raw data need uploading to internet servers. Raw data stays on phone—only processing results or small anonymised data synced out.
Speed Depends on network quality—latency. Asking a question requires "network loading." Instant response, no latency. Unlocking, photo searching, offline translation—in the blink of an eye.
Privacy & Security Your data needs to "leave home"—passing through public networks, potential leakage risk. Data does not leave the "avatar"—fundamentally avoiding eavesdropping and interception during transmission.
Offline capability Basically no. No network ≈ AI features do not work. Core features work completely offline—adapts to more scenarios (underground car parks, planes, wilderness).
Power & Data Usage Needs continuous network communication—relatively power and data hungry. Reduces frequent communication with cloud—more power and data efficient.
Current examples Early speech recognition, AI image generation requiring complex rendering, large-parameter language model conversations. Face/fingerprint unlocking, photo face categorisation, offline translation, some voice commands, keyboard predictions.

See the difference? On-device AI perfectly echoes the previous chapter's security appeal of "protecting your digital avatar." It builds the "avatar's" boundary walls more solidly and intelligently. Data and computation stay within the walls as much as possible—minimising risk and latency.

The future "large models" are also evolving toward on-device. Although today's complete ChatGPT-style conversations cannot run on a small phone, its "mini refined version" can already reside on phones, handling some local tasks. In the future, your phone butler, note apps, and even game NPCs might all be driven by your privately carried on-device large model—it completely knows your habits and serves only you.

In the previous two chapters, we built a secure "boundary wall" for our phone and witnessed its increasingly intelligent "AI brain" within those walls. It is like your customised "digital servant," or a set of freely combinable "digital LEGO."

🤖 What is Automation? What Can It Do for Me?

Simply put, automation packages those frequent, fixed-pattern repetitive operations into an "instruction set" and lets the phone run them automatically when conditions are met.

It is not magic—it is logic. Its core usually contains three elements:

  1. Trigger condition: When to start? Like "when I connect to home Wi-Fi," "every day at 8 AM," "when I open an app," "when I arrive at a location (geofence)."
  2. Action: Specifically what to do? Like "turn on living room smart lights," "lower media volume," "send a preset SMS to someone," "record today's step count to notes."
  3. (Optional) Logic: Need to judge? Like "if it is a workday, play news; if weekend, play music."

What can it do for you? Imagine these scenarios:

  • Life efficiency: Auto turn on lights when arriving home, auto connect Wi-Fi and play relaxation playlist; before bed, auto enable Do Not Disturb, dim screen and set alarm.
  • Work assistance: Auto mute when entering office area and switch to work to-do list app; after meeting ends, auto save recording to cloud and email share to colleagues.
  • Personalised care: When rain forecast is detected, auto pop up "remember to bring umbrella" on lock screen; when battery below 20%, auto enable ultra power-saving mode and remind you to charge.
  • Fun uses: Stick NFC tags on car—tap to auto navigate home and play car music; scan specific QR code to auto join Wi-Fi and show today's menu.

Automation frees your hands and attention—transforming your phone from a passive tool into an proactive, thoughtful life companion.

📱 "Euthanasia" for Old Phones: Safe Selling or Gifting Guide

Handling an old phone is absolutely not the same as throwing away an old water cup. It is more like handling a mini safe full of all your diaries, letters, financial receipts and house keys. Direct formatting? Factory reset? These operations may just be tearing off the "safe's" label, while the contents inside are still quietly lying in some corner of the storage chip, waiting to be "awakened" by professional software.

Core principle: Data cleaning is not "deletion," but "overwriting."

1. Step One: Personal "Big Cleanup"
  • Before operating, please be sure to complete one last full data backup (review Chapter 16). This is like packing up precious items for storage before moving, got it?
  • Manually log out of all logged-in accounts, especially WeChat, Alipay, online banking Apps. Remove this phone's login authorisation from the device management list. This is equivalent to wiping the fingerprint lock on the safe.
  • If the phone supports it, you can remove or unpair all Bluetooth devices and smart wearable devices.
2. Step Two: Execute Ultimate "Formatting"—Encrypted Erasure
  • Modern phones (especially iOS and higher versions of Android) "restore factory settings" usually automatically include an "encrypted erasure" process. It overwrites the original encryption key with random data, making old data become unreadable garbled text. This is much safer than simply deleting files, remember that?
  • Confirm before doing: Find "Reset" or "Restore Factory Settings" option in settings, carefully read the instructions, ensure it includes "Delete All Data" or "Clear All Content" prompt.
  • Remove external storage: If your phone has an SD card, be sure to physically remove it and handle separately (format or physical destruction).
3. Step Three: Final "Camouflage"
  • After restoring factory settings, the phone will enter the guide interface like new. At this point, do not activate! Do not log in!
  • You can choose to skip all setup steps, then fill the phone storage space again with irrelevant pictures, videos and other junk files, then execute factory reset again. This "fill then delete" operation can further reduce the possibility of original data being recovered.

Completing the above three steps, your old phone has finally completed the "euthanasia" of data, and can safely embark on its next journey.

🏛️ Digital Legacy: How to Place Your Social Soul?

If cleaning the phone is handling "physical legacy," then digital legacy is handling your "social soul." Those Moments, Weibo, family photos in cloud drives, purchased e-books and music... they constitute all the memories of your digital life. But when we go offline, how should these assets be handled?

The reality is: Platform rules > Personal wishes. Now, domestic and foreign mainstream platforms have hugely different policies for digital legacy handling, most without clear, convenient inheritance processes. So, planning ahead is especially important.

1. "Legacy Contact" and "Memorial Account"
  • Apple provides "Legacy Contact" feature. You can designate one or more trusted contacts. When you pass away, they can apply to access your iCloud account data (such as photos, notes, memos, etc.) through access keys and death certificate.
  • Google has "Inactive Account Manager." You can set a period (such as 3, 6, 12 months of inactivity), then Google will try to contact you. If confirming the account is unused, it will send data to your designated contacts according to your preset settings, or directly delete the account.
  • Facebook allows you to set your account as "Memorial Account." Designate a "Legacy Contact," this contact can manage your memorial homepage after you pass away (post pinned posts on behalf, reply to friend requests, etc.), but cannot directly log into your account or view private messages.
2. Manual Backup: The Most Primitive "Family Heirloom"

For truly precious data (such as family photos and videos), the most reliable method is still regularly downloading them to local hard drives or home NAS, and informing family of storage location and access method. This is like putting old photos into physical albums, the certainty of inheritance is highest.

3. A Simple "Digital Will"

You do not need to write it formally. You can list the following information in a safe note (or tell family):

  • Core account list: WeChat, email, main social platforms, cloud drives, financial accounts, etc.
  • Important data locations: Point out which cloud service or local device your most cherished photos and documents are stored in.
  • Your wishes: Whether you hope the account is permanently deleted, converted to memorial status, or specific data is passed to someone.

Planning digital legacy is not unlucky, but a responsible digital citizen behaviour. It ensures your digital footprint can be respected and handled according to your wishes, rather than being forgotten in corners of the network, or causing unnecessary family troubles.

♻️ The Last Journey of Environmental Protection: Recycling of Electronic Products

An old phone that has been safely cleaned, its physical life still has three mainstream paths:

  1. Official trade-in/recycling: This is the most recommended way. Apple, Huawei, Xiaomi and other brands all have official trade-in or recycling programs. Advantages include:
    • Standardised process, data security is guaranteed (usually require you to wipe data yourself beforehand).
    • Transparent valuation, can directly deduct from new phone payment.
    • Environmental processing, official partners will professionally disassemble devices, valuable parts are recycled, harmful substances are harmlessly treated.
    • May get coupons or gifts, direct cash out.
  2. Reputable recycling platforms or offline stores: Choose reputable large recycling platforms or offline digital recycling stores. Be sure to complete data cleaning on-site, and let the other party issue a recycling receipt. Absolutely avoid street vendors without qualifications.
  3. Second career (gift or backup):
    • Give to family/friends: As "senior phone" for elders, learning device for children, or family backup phone.
    • Dedicated device: Convert to dedicated car navigation, smart home control centre (always plugged into dock controlling lights, speakers), or security camera (using idle phone + monitoring App).
    • Donate: Some public welfare organisations will accept still-usable old phones, after processing provide to groups in need.

Whichever path you choose, letting old devices enter the formal recycling circulation system is reducing "e-waste" pollution for the Earth. What you phase out may be the beginning of another digital life.

🌌 Next-Generation Devices: What Will It Become?

Future devices must first solve the problem of "fixed form." A glass slab has ruled us for over a decade, and tech industry designers have long been restless.

Foldable Phones: An Oversized "Phablet" or a Folded "Wallet"?

This wonderful contradiction has already walked into reality. Imagine, you are scrolling through Weibo with one hand on the subway, unfold and immediately get a tablet-comparable view to binge shows or process documents. It is like an exquisite Japanese folding fan, switching between different life scenarios with opening and closing. But joking aside, it is essentially forcing the choice question of "should I buy a phone or a tablet" into a "two-in-one" fill-in-the-blank question, and also gifting an "anxiety about screen creases" bonus thinking question. It perhaps tells us that what future devices pursue is not "bigger," but "more appropriate form."

AR Glasses: Open a "Second Desktop" Before Your Eyes

If foldable devices are "transformation," then augmented reality (AR) glasses are "overlay." It no longer attempts to replace that slab in your pocket, but wants to become a filter through which you see the world—navigation arrows directly projected on the road ahead, friend's chat window floating beside your coffee cup, foreign menu you want to translate automatically overlaid with Chinese. Sounds like sci-fi movies, but major tech giants have already set it as the next "ten-year bet." Its ambition is to make digital information as ubiquitous as air, yet exist "non-intrusively." Of course, the premise is it must be light enough (not like diving goggles anymore), beautiful enough (not making you look like a cyberpunk street interview subject), and must solve an ultimate social dilemma—when we are all wearing glasses to communicate, how to judge whether the other person is really looking at me, or browsing the weather widget above my head?

Brain-Computer Interfaces: Will "Thought Typing" Be the "Nine-Key" of the Next Decade?

The Matrix and Ready Player One have implanted the ultimate vision into our cultural genes—eventually, no external devices needed, thought itself will become the interaction medium. Now, some cutting-edge experiments have made electrode patches as thin as temporary tattoos, using thoughts to control game characters or type simple words, already preliminarily feasible. The future it depicts is an ultimate liberation of interaction: paralysed patients can use thoughts to type and communicate, creators can skip keyboard and mouse to directly "download" inspiration into documents. The vision is immensely grand, but reality reminds us this path is much longer than evolving from "brick phones" to "smartphones." Protecting the absolute privacy of personal thoughts will be an ethical proposition several orders of magnitude higher than the App permission management we discuss now. (For our generation, perhaps first learning to proficiently use the automation tools discussed in this chapter is more practical.)

🤖️ The "Invisibility" of Phones and the New Identity Cornerstone

Setting aside the cool device forms, another future evolution direction of phones may be "invisibility." It is not about changing into other shapes, but wanting to become an ubiquitous "behind-the-scenes mastermind," becoming the sole foundation of your digital identity.

This matter was seeded in the previous chapter. When you set "digital legacy contact," enable "account trusteeship," you are actually saying: "This device and this online identity are 'my' digital extension. Please handle it properly when I go offline."

In the future, this "identity binding" will become more underlying and smooth:

  • Cloud-based two-factor authentication everywhere: When you approach the shopping cart checkout, the system no longer needs SMS verification codes, but directly initiates biometric (fingerprint/face/voiceprint) inquiry to your cloud identity, instantly completing "you are you" authentication.
  • Digital legacy becomes factory default: When activating a new phone, the system may warmly ask like "whether to enable backup": "Do you wish to preset a safe inheritance plan for your most precious digital assets (such as encrypted albums, documents, social accounts)?" It is no longer a niche advanced feature, but a default service accessible to everyone.
  • From "device-centred" to "identity-centred": Today, your data is scattered in phones, tablets, computers. In the future, your cloud personal data vault is the centre. All devices: unfolded foldable screens, AR glasses you are wearing, even smart mirrors at home, are just different "displays" or "sensors" reading this "central identity." The phone is no longer the "home" of data, but your most intimate, important "portable door"—this is important.

This brings huge convenience, and also a huge and urgent reminder: Protecting your main account (Apple ID/Google account/WeChat, etc.) will be ten thousand times more important than protecting the phone itself. Because losing a phone is just losing a door, having your main account stolen is equivalent to someone copying all your rooms' master keys and your digital DNA, do not get that wrong.

🧐 Ethical Reflection: Are We Still the Phone's Master?

No matter how devices evolve, an ultimate question always exists: Are we "gods" empowered by technology, or "brains in vats" fed by algorithms? Philosophers call it "alienation"—we created tools, but are ultimately reversely defined and shaped by tools.

Think about it:

  • When algorithms know better than you what you want to see (or even want to buy), how much of your "liking" is spontaneous, how much is the result of being carefully "recommended" by computing power?
  • When you cannot help lighting up the screen to glance every few minutes (even without notifications), is this your habit, or "addictive behaviour" designed by the phone through instant feedback mechanisms?
  • Exercise: Next time friends gather for dinner, observe whether there is a "phone wall" moment (everyone is looking down at phones). Try stacking everyone's phone screens down in the centre of the table, feel the brief "withdrawal reaction" in the first few minutes and how the subsequent real conversation is different.

Of course, completely rejecting technology is turning back the wheel of history. Those Apps claiming to make you completely "quit phones" (sometimes, they themselves are quite funny) often only bring brief novelty, but cannot last. A true healthy relationship is not about "quitting," but about "control" and "symbiosis."

📦 Appendix A: New Phone Unboxing "Nursery-Level" Process

Do not rush to play when you get a new phone! Follow these steps, very steady:

1. Unboxing Inspection "Trilogy":

  • Check appearance: Inspect body and screen for scratches, bumps.
  • Verify information: Match IMEI/serial number on box (in About Phone) with invoice.
  • Test functions: Make a call to test earpiece and microphone, take a photo to check camera, connect to Wi-Fi and mobile network, got it?

2. Activation and Initial Setup "Key Reminders":

  • Network: First connect to stable Wi-Fi, avoid using mobile data to download updates, it costs money!
  • Account: Log in to your Apple ID, Huawei account, Xiaomi account, etc. Remember, this is your phone's "ID card," finding phone and cloud backup all depend on it.
  • Basic settings: While at it, turn on "Find My Phone," set a reliable lock screen password (stop using 123456!). Set screen brightness to auto, comfortable.

3. Installing Apps "Safety First":

  • Official stores first: Apple use App Store, Android use phone's built-in app store (like Huawei App Market, Xiaomi App Store).
  • Do not grant permissions carelessly: When installation prompts permission requests, especially for contacts, SMS, location, microphone—think clearly "does this App really need it?" If not, turn it off.
